## Executive Summary

Geraldo Lunas Campos, a 55-year-old Cuban father of four, died on January 3, 2026 at Camp East Montana — the nation's largest immigration detention facility, built on the Fort Bliss Army base in El Paso, Texas. The El Paso County Medical Examiner ruled his death a **homicide**, finding he died from **"asphyxia due to neck and torso compression."**

Eyewitnesses — fellow detainees who submitted sworn declarations — described guards slamming Campos to the ground and choking him after he asked for his asthma medication. One witness heard him cry out "I can't breathe" before silence fell. Another heard a guard tell him "Shut up or we're going to make you faint."

ICE's narrative changed three times: first claiming staff "observed him in distress," then claiming he attempted suicide, and finally — after the homicide ruling — emphasizing his criminal history rather than addressing how he died.

**Key finding:** This is a confirmed homicide by guards at the nation's largest detention center, with eyewitness testimony corroborating the autopsy findings. ICE attempted to cover it up as a suicide. After the medical examiner ruled it homicide, ICE redirected the next body (Victor Manuel Diaz, Jan 14) to a military hospital that doesn't release autopsy reports publicly.

**Detention History:**
- Arrested July 2025 in Rochester, NY during immigration enforcement action
- Transferred to Camp East Montana in September 2025
- Detained approximately 3 months before death

## 2. INCIDENT TIMELINE

### January 3, 2026 — The Killing

**What witnesses describe:**

1. Campos was in line for medication
2. He became "disruptive" (DHS characterization) — witnesses say he was **requesting his asthma medication**
3. Guards attempted to place him in the segregation unit (solitary confinement)
4. Campos refused, stating he didn't have access to his medication

**Eyewitness: Henry Bolano (detainee)**
- Heard Campos request asthma medication
- Heard a guard tell Campos: **"Shut up or we're going to make you faint"**
- Heard Campos cry out: **"Let go of me. You're asphyxiating me"**
- Then: **silence**

**Eyewitness: Santos Jesús Flores, 47 (detainee, El Salvador)**
- Watched through the window of his cell in the special housing unit
- Saw **at least five guards** fighting with Campos
- Saw guards **choking** Campos
- Heard **"the slamming of a person's body against the floor or the wall"**
- Heard Campos scream: **"I can't breathe"** ("no puedo respirar")
- "I could hear them trying to revive him, but they could not keep him alive"

**Associated Press witness account:**
- Campos was **handcuffed** while "at least five guards held him down and one put an arm around his neck and squeezed until he was unconscious"

## 3. AUTOPSY FINDINGS

### El Paso County Medical Examiner

**Examiner:** Dr. Adam Gonzalez, Deputy Medical Examiner for El Paso County

**Cause of Death:** Asphyxia due to neck and torso compression

**Manner of Death:** **HOMICIDE**

**Physical Evidence:**
- Abrasions on chest and knees
- Neck hemorrhages
- **Petechial hemorrhages** (tiny blood spots from burst capillaries) in eyelids and neck skin
- Contusions consistent with physical restraint
- Neck injuries suggesting pressure from a hand or knee

**Determination:** Campos died "while being physically restrained by law enforcement"

## 4. ICE'S THREE NARRATIVES

### Narrative 1: Medical Distress (January 9)
- ICE stated staff "observed him in distress" and provided "lifesaving measures"
- **No mention of any altercation with guards**

### Narrative 2: Suicide Attempt (Following Week)
- After AP questions, DHS spokesperson Tricia McLaughlin amended the account
- Claimed Campos attempted suicide and guards tried to save him
- Statement: "Campos violently resisted the security staff and continued to attempt to take his life"

### Narrative 3: Criminal Background Deflection (Post-Autopsy)
- After the homicide ruling, McLaughlin emphasized Campos's criminal history
- **Did not address the autopsy findings of homicide by asphyxiation**
- Did not explain how a man requesting asthma medication ended up dead from neck compression

### The Pattern
Each narrative shift came in response to new evidence contradicting the previous story. The progression from "medical distress" → "suicide" → "he was a criminal" mirrors a cover-up pattern seen across multiple ICE deaths.

## 5. CAMP EAST MONTANA

### Facility Profile
- **Location:** Fort Bliss Army base, El Paso, Texas
- **Type:** Tent-based detention facility
- **Capacity:** Over 2,700 detainees — **nation's largest immigration detention center**
- **Opened:** August 2025
- **Cost:** $1.2 billion contract
- **Operator:** Acquisition Logistics LLC — a private contractor headquartered in a **single-family home in Richmond, Virginia** with **no prior corrections facility experience**
- **Historical context:** Fort Bliss was a **Japanese internment site during WWII**

### Three Deaths in Six Weeks
1. **Francisco Gaspar-Andres** (48, Guatemala): December 3, 2025 — suspected liver/kidney failure
2. **Geraldo Lunas Campos** (55, Cuba): January 3, 2026 — **HOMICIDE** (asphyxia by guards)
3. **Victor Manuel Diaz** (36, Nicaragua): January 14, 2026 — "presumed suicide" (body sent to military hospital to avoid independent autopsy)

### Documented Conditions (ACLU, December 2025)
- Officer beatings and sexual abuse against detainees
- Coercive threats forcing deportations to third countries
- Medical neglect and insufficient food
- Denial of meaningful legal access

## 6. AUTOPSY MANIPULATION

After Dr. Gonzalez ruled Lunas Campos's death a homicide, ICE took extraordinary action with the next body:

- **Victor Manuel Diaz** died January 14 at the same facility
- His body was sent to **William Beaumont Army Medical Center** (military hospital at Fort Bliss) instead of the El Paso County Medical Examiner
- William Beaumont **does not release autopsy reports to the public** — only to family and investigators
- The El Paso County ME's office told Diaz's attorney they **attempted to take custody of the body** but "a disagreement with the federal government over jurisdiction delayed the process"
- Diaz's family was **never notified** of the transfer to the military hospital

**This represents deliberate circumvention of independent civilian oversight after that oversight produced a homicide ruling.**

## 8. PATTERN ANALYSIS

### "I Can't Breathe" — An American Pattern
- Campos's cry of "no puedo respirar" ("I can't breathe") echoes George Floyd, Eric Garner, and others killed by positional asphyxia during law enforcement restraint
- Five guards restraining one handcuffed man requesting his medication
- Neck compression causing death — a known lethal technique many agencies have banned

### Cover-Up Pattern
1. Initial narrative contains no mention of force
2. When questioned, narrative shifts to blame the victim (suicide, resistance)
3. When autopsy contradicts, narrative shifts to criminal background
4. Next death: body redirected to avoid independent medical examination

### Facility Accountability Pattern
- $1.2 billion facility operated by company with no corrections experience
- Three deaths in six weeks
- ACLU documented beatings, sexual abuse, medical neglect
- Historical resonance: detention on former Japanese internment site
